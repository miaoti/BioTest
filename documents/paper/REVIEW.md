# Review of "BioTest: Metamorphic Testing for Bioinformatics File-Format Parsers"

## 1. Headline judgment

**Reject.** The paper's central empirical claim — "grammar-driven metamorphic testing comes close to coverage-guided fuzzing under bounded per-SUT effort" — rests on (a) a mutation-adequacy proxy that the authors themselves admit correlates only at $r{\approx}0.7$ with real faults, on a six-cell benchmark with $n{=}4$ reps, while (b) a real-bug benchmark exists in the artefact (`compares/results/bug_bench/PHASE4_FINAL_REPORT.md`, 32 bugs, BioTest finds 13/32) but is excluded from the paper, and (c) the corpus that is graded for mutation adequacy is itself selected by a kill-aware greedy set-cover that uses mutation-kill information as the optimisation signal (`corpus_minimize.py::minimize_by_kills`). Every one of these is independently reject-grade for a top-tier venue; together they collapse the paper's headline numbers into circular evidence.

## 2. Summary of contribution (in my words)

The paper packages a five-phase, LLM-assisted metamorphic-testing pipeline for VCF and SAM parsers, defines a "thirteen-rank" stack of input-generation operators (seven metamorphic, six augmentation), and grades the resulting corpus against five coverage-guided fuzzers using mutation kills as the adequacy criterion on six (SUT, format) cells. The authors claim parity-within-a-few-percentage-points on four cells, attribute one large deficit to information-theoretic dominance of coverage feedback and one small lead to oracle-strength asymmetry, and frame the artefact as the first SUT-agnostic MR-based parser-tester comparable in user effort to in-process fuzzers. The contribution claim oscillates: the abstract sells "metamorphic testing pipeline whose per-SUT user effort is bounded by the fuzz-tool baseline"; the design section now admits the user authors per-SUT shims, runners, and a coverage-filter YAML stanza; the conclusion shifts to "one round-trip MR plus SUT-agnostic generator." This is not one contribution stated three ways — it is three different framings of the same artefact, each tailored to deflect a different anticipated objection.

## 3. Major issues (severity-ranked)

### M1. Mutation-adequacy proxy is the only headline number, while a real-bug benchmark sitting in the artefact is excluded from the paper.
*Where:* `evaluation_intro.tex` lines 13–17 ("Real-fault detection on a historical-parser-bug benchmark is in scope for follow-up work"); `threats.tex` Construct paragraph; entire absence from `mutation_table.tex`. *Why it is a problem:* The artefact directory `compares/results/bug_bench/PHASE4_FINAL_REPORT.md` contains a 32-bug, 8-tool, 4-SUT real-bug detection comparison in which BioTest detects 13/32 bugs and is the only tool with anything close to broad coverage. The paper is grading itself on mutation kills (a $r{\approx}0.7$ proxy validated only on Defects4J Java) when the authors *already have* the real-bug data the field would actually want to see. Either (i) the real-bug numbers are weaker than the mutation numbers and the paper is choosing the more flattering metric, or (ii) the authors believe the bug-bench is too immature to publish — in which case it should not exist as a finished `PHASE4_FINAL_REPORT.md`, and the omission is a presentation choice, not a methodological necessity. Either reading is reject-grade. *Minimum fix:* Promote bug-bench results to a primary table in the Evaluation; if some cells are not yet ready, report partial results with cell-counts disclosed — but a paper that *has* the data and *publishes only the proxy* is not a top-tier submission.

### M2. The mutation-kill signal is used to select the corpus that is graded by mutation kill — circular evidence.
*Where:* This is *not* in the paper. It is in `Flow.md` lines 1418–1429 (Refinement B: kill-aware fitness, `corpus_minimize.py::minimize_by_kills`). The paper's Section 5.4 fairness recipe ("identical mutation engine, identical scope, identical kill semantics") goes silent on how each tool's corpus reaches the engine. *Why it is a problem:* Flow.md says the default corpus minimisation strategy on Python SUTs is `kill_aware`, which mutates source code, computes kill-sets per candidate file, and runs greedy set-cover to maximise *mutation kill coverage* on the surviving subset. The protocol is then to grade that surviving subset on the same mutation engine. The advertised mutation score is therefore a measurement of how well a kill-aware optimisation procedure optimises kills — not a measurement of how well the testing tool generates faults. This is true cmin circularity, exactly the trap that Vikram et al. ISSTA'23 (which the authors cite as inspiration) issues a warning against using as an evaluation signal rather than as a guidance signal. *Minimum fix:* Either (i) report results without `minimize_by_kills` (use `outcome_fingerprint` only, on every cell, including Python SUTs), or (ii) clearly disclose this in the fairness-recipe paragraph and explain why it is fair to apply this signal asymmetrically (Python only, while Java/Rust/C++ fall back to outcome-fingerprint per Flow.md line 1425). The current paper does neither, which is reject-grade.

### M3. n=4 reps with $\sigma{=}0.00$ on multiple cells means the statistical battery is theatre.
*Where:* `mutation_table.tex` lines 27–33 (BioTest seqan3 $97.44{\pm}0.00$, libFuzzer seqan3 $90.57{\pm}0.00$, BioTest biopython full-population $25.81{\pm}0.00$, htsjdk/VCF Pure-Random $0.00{\pm}0.00$); `mutation_score_section.tex` line 99 acknowledges "both samples are deterministic at $\sigma{=}0.00$, so $n{=}4$ is not informing variance." *Why it is a problem:* If both populations have zero variance, $\hat{A}_{12}$ and Mann–Whitney U are not meaningful inferential statistics — they are deterministic ordinal comparisons of single values, dressed up as statistics. The minimum exact two-sided $p$ at $n_1{=}n_2{=}4$ is $1/35{\approx}0.029$, which is the *minimum* not a measurement. Pre-Holm/Bonferroni correction at six cells, $0.029$ becomes $0.174$. The paper reports $\hat{A}_{12}{=}0.000$ and $\hat{A}_{12}{=}1.000$ as if these were findings; with $\sigma{=}0.00$ they are simply tautologies. *Minimum fix:* Either (i) increase $n$ to ${\geq}10$ where the kill computation has any non-determinism, or (ii) drop the inferential apparatus entirely and frame the results as observations on small samples. The current presentation lets a small-effect cell ($-1.3$pp) and a large-effect cell ($+6.87$pp) both report $\hat{A}_{12}$ at the same precision, which is misleading.

### M4. Researcher-degree-of-freedom in MR curation is not blinded and not quantified.
*Where:* `implementation.tex` lines 25–26 ("relations that fail on a hand-curated seed corpus are discarded before admission to the registry"); `threats.tex` lines 67–79 acknowledges this. *Why it is a problem:* The authors prompt an LLM for MR candidates, run them on a hand-prepared seed corpus that the same authors curate, discard the candidates that fail, and the survivors are the MRs that produce the headline numbers. There is no blind. The seed corpus is not pre-registered. There is no audit of how many MRs were rejected, why, or what their rejection rate was on each iteration of the prompt loop. This is exactly the data leakage that empirical-software-engineering reviewers are trained to surface. The threats section says "the resulting researcher-degree-of-freedom is not fully quantifiable" — and then the paper proceeds anyway. *Minimum fix:* Pre-register the seed corpus *before* MR mining, run the curation gate as a public CI job whose discard log is part of the artefact, and report (a) the number of MR candidates proposed, (b) the number passed, (c) the per-MR contribution to the kill rate. Without those numbers, the LLM-mined-MR claim is not falsifiable.

### M5. C1 (SUT-agnostic) and C2 (per-SUT user effort bounded by fuzz-tool baseline) jointly contradict the abstract's "no per-SUT metamorphic relation, no per-SUT oracle predicate, no curated seed corpus."
*Where:* `abstract.tex` lines 14–16 vs. `design.tex` C2 lines 57–90 vs. `Flow.md` §11–12 ("onboarding checklist: harness + runner + coverage scope YAML; without item 3, Phase D feedback signal is garbage"). *Why it is a problem:* The abstract still says "no curated seed corpus," but C2 admits "seeds are LLM-synthesised in Rank~1 and harvested from upstream collections in Rank~2," and Flow.md §11.7 documents `seeds/<fmt>/` and a `seeds/ref/toy.fa` toy reference for CRAM. The abstract still says "no per-SUT oracle predicate," but `harness/cpp/biotest_harness.cpp` and `harnesses/java/BioTestHarness.java` *are* per-SUT oracle code (they encode the canonical-JSON projection that the consensus oracle hashes). C2 has been rewritten to thread this needle by saying "the same kind of artefact a fuzzer requires," but the abstract has not been updated and the conclusion has not been updated. The paper says one thing in the abstract and a different thing in §3, with the latter contradicting the former by name. *Minimum fix:* Rewrite the abstract to match C2 verbatim. The current "no curated seed corpus" is wrong on the artefact's own terms.

### M6. The fairness recipe's "identical budget" is not equivalent work — it asymmetrically punishes high-iteration-cost tools.
*Where:* `mutation_score_section.tex` lines 100–106 (the AFL++ vs libFuzzer 17pp gap explained as "in-process vs fork-server iteration cost"); `design.tex` C 5 fairness paragraph. *Why it is a problem:* The authors themselves disclose that on seqan3, libFuzzer and AFL++ differ by 17pp on the *same SUT and same engine* purely because of iteration-cost asymmetry under the wall-clock budget. By the authors' own logic, this means "identical wall-clock budget" is *not* fair when iteration costs differ across tools. But the rest of the paper assumes the budget is fair. If the budget is unfair on seqan3 (where it produces a 17pp artefact), why is the budget fair on htsjdk (where Jazzer's compile-test JVM warmup also produces an iteration-cost asymmetry that the paper does not measure)? This is not an internal threat — it is a falsifying observation that the paper buries in a caveat. *Minimum fix:* Run a second comparison under iteration-count parity (identical number of corpus inputs reach the parser per cell). If the seqan3 lead survives that, the lead is real; if it does not, the lead is a budget artefact and the paper's headline must be retracted.

### M7. The seqan3 +6.87pp lead via "oracle-strength asymmetry" is incompatible with the kill-computation pipeline.
*Where:* `mutation_score_section.tex` lines 86–106 ("our oracle hashes parse outcome (record count plus exception class) where libFuzzer's crash-or-hang oracle counts these as expected … typed-exception flips … invisible to a crash oracle"). *Why it is a problem:* In the fairness recipe the kill semantics are *identical*: a mutant is killed if any input from the tool's corpus produces a divergent outcome from baseline under the *same* mutation engine. The mutation engine, not the testing tool's oracle, decides what counts as a kill. If the kill predicate is parse-success-flip ∨ canonical-JSON divergence ∨ crash-class flip (as `design.tex` C3 says), then this same predicate is applied to the libFuzzer corpus too. So the *same exception-class flips* that BioTest's corpus catches will also count as kills if libFuzzer's corpus contains an input that triggers one. The advantage cannot live in the oracle, because the oracle is the engine's, not the tool's. The advantage must therefore live in the *corpus* — but the corpus is being graded the same way as everyone else's. The information-theoretic story (Section 5 Background) and the seqan3-asymmetry story are mutually inconsistent on the engine semantics. *Minimum fix:* Reconcile these. Either (a) the engine's kill predicate is in fact crash-only (in which case the seqan3 explanation is correct and the paper has been describing the kill semantics incorrectly throughout), or (b) the engine's kill predicate matches the digest oracle (in which case the seqan3 story is wrong and the lead must be re-explained from the corpus side). Both cannot be true.

### M8. The biopython 32.5pp gap is "explained" by an entropy claim that is never computed.
*Where:* `mutation_score_section.tex` lines 50–64 ("the input space of structurally-valid byte-corrupted SAM records is $O(2^{8k})$ for record length $k$, and spec-driven enumeration achieves vanishing yield without coverage-feedback to prune"). *Why it is a problem:* The $O(2^{8k})$ figure is the trivial upper bound on byte sequences of length $k$; it is not the entropy of the *structurally valid* subset that actually reaches the parser, and it is certainly not the entropy of the kill-relevant subset. To make the information-theoretic claim, the paper would need to (a) measure the empirical entropy of biopython-accepted byte-strings under each tool's corpus, (b) measure Atheris's coverage-feedback marginal gain in bits, and (c) show the gain is consistent with the 32.5pp deficit. None of this is done. The argument is post-hoc rationalisation in the language of information theory. *Minimum fix:* Either compute the entropies, or downgrade to "we observe a deficit consistent with prior reports of coverage-feedback advantage on byte-content-lenient parsers." The current "consistent with the information-theoretic prediction that…" wording falsely implies a quantitative match.

### M9. The "thirteen ranks" framing hides that augmentation ranks (12, 13) carry the corpus into competitive territory while only the metamorphic ranks (1–7) match the paper's stated theoretical contribution.
*Where:* `design.tex` §3.3 enumerates the ranks; `mutation_score_section.tex` paragraph on biopython mentions Rank-12/13 augmentation explicitly; the abstract bills the contribution as "a thirteen-rank corpus-generation stack." *Why it is a problem:* Per the design itself, Ranks 12 and 13 are *not metamorphic* — Rank-13 is "intentionally not metamorphic" and consumed only by mutation-kill grading. The headline mutation score therefore fuses metamorphic-MR contribution with a non-metamorphic byte-fuzz contribution. The paper does not report the metamorphic-only mutation score on each cell. The result is that a reader cannot tell how much of the kill rate comes from MRs (the paper's claimed contribution) versus from the lenient byte fuzzer (which is just a stripped-down Atheris). *Minimum fix:* Report Ranks 1–7 alone vs. Ranks 1–13 on every cell. If Ranks 1–7 alone are uncompetitive, the paper's actual contribution is "we add a byte fuzzer to a metamorphic test loop," which is a different, smaller paper.

### M10. LLM contribution is not isolated — the paper does not say how many MRs the LLM produced or what each contributed.
*Where:* `implementation.tex` Phase B paragraph; `Flow.md` line 615 ("3 enforced MRs"); the paper's prose suggests dozens. *Why it is a problem:* Flow.md reports the MR registry stabilised at *3 enforced MRs* (with ablation expansions later); the paper rhetorically refers to "the LLM-mined relation catalogue" as if it were a substantial set. The LLM's contribution to the headline numbers is therefore (i) unquantified, (ii) on Flow.md's own count, very small in cardinality. The thirteen-rank stack also includes hand-coded transforms (Ranks 9, 11, 12 are spec-rule-driven, not LLM-mined). The paper conflates LLM-mined and hand-coded contributions throughout. *Minimum fix:* Per-MR ablation on at least one cell, and a count: how many MRs were active at evaluation time, how many were LLM-mined (vs. hand-curated), and what the kill rate is when LLM-mined MRs are removed.

### M11. Pure-Random as "floor reference" is a calibration of parser strictness, not of the recipe's discrimination.
*Where:* `design.tex` lines 351–361, claim that Pure-Random scores from $0.00\%$ on htsjdk/VCF to $7.19\%$ on seqan3/SAM "refute the hypothesis that the recipe is measuring something other than corpus quality." *Why it is a problem:* The argument is that Pure-Random's score variation across cells implies the recipe distinguishes corpora. Counter-argument: the variation can equally be explained by parser strictness — htsjdk/VCF rejects any random byte stream at the header line, so Pure-Random scores 0; seqan3 accepts more permissive inputs and so random bytes occasionally reach mutated lines. That is a property of *the parsers*, not of the *recipe*. The 0.00% Pure-Random floor on htsjdk/VCF tells us only that the parser's header is strict, not that BioTest's 32.53% is meaningful in that context. *Minimum fix:* Compute, per cell, the Pure-Random pass-rate (fraction of inputs accepted past the header). Then express each tool's score as a function of that. Without it, the floor is uninformative.

### M12. Six cells, two formats, four parser languages — external validity claim does not hold up.
*Where:* `abstract.tex` and `mutation_score_section.tex` make general statements; `threats.tex` lines 81–93 admit the result does not extend to BCF/BAM/CRAM. *Why it is a problem:* The threats section walks back the title-and-abstract claim. The title says "Bioinformatics File-Format Parsers"; the threats say BCF/BAM/CRAM (the binary counterparts the field actually uses) are out of scope. That is an enormous fraction of the deployed parser surface area. The corpus of generalisability is in fact: VCF text and SAM text, six cells, four languages, all parsing into nearly-identical record models. This is not "Bioinformatics File-Format Parsers"; it is "Two Text Formats in Genomics." *Minimum fix:* Retitle to scope appropriately, or run on at least one binary cell (which would surface the additional engineering BioTest cannot trivially absorb).

### M13. Reproducibility: a single Docker image is claimed but no public artefact-availability commitment is made.
*Where:* `implementation.tex` lines 76–85 ("biotest-bench:latest" image). *Why it is a problem:* No public registry URL, no Docker-Hub digest, no Zenodo DOI, no artefact-evaluation badge intent. The paper claims reproducibility; a third-party reviewer cannot pull the image as advertised. The thread of citations to Mull's deb-package incompatibility is the kind of detail that *only* matters if the reader has the artefact — without it, the paragraph reads as authorial defensiveness. *Minimum fix:* Cite a public artefact (Docker Hub URL, Zenodo, GitHub release tag) and commit to the artefact-evaluation track at submission time.

## 4. Minor issues

- **Citation gap on grammar fuzzers:** Csmith (2011) is cited; Nautilus and Superion are cited; but **Gopinath et al. (Mining Input Grammars, ESEC/FSE'20), Pham et al. (Smart Greybox Fuzzing, ICSE'19), and Padhye PerfFuzz/RLCheck** are not. The related-work section claims to position against grammar-aware fuzzing without engaging the post-2019 wave.
- **Notation inconsistency:** `design.tex` writes the metamorphic relation as $\mathit{out}(\mathit{parse}(x)) \equiv \mathit{out}(\mathit{parse}(T(x)))$ (line 144) while the introduction writes "identity relation on the parsed structure" (line 41) and the figure caption says "metamorphic equivalence." Three terms for the same relation.
- **TikZ figure is busy without clarifying the user's contract.** Figure 1 has 13 nodes, three legends inline, and a feedback loop that crosses two rows. The "green trapezium" highlights the user-supplied shim, but the figure's information density makes the C2-vs-C1 distinction hard to read at print scale.
- **PR/PoV vs. mutation-kill conflation in `mutation_table.tex` footnote** ($\dagger$, $\ddag$): the explanation of why Jazzer reports $n{=}2$ on htsjdk/SAM ("two of four reps produced corpora that did not pass the pre-grading validity gate") raises an immediate question the paper does not answer — what is the validity gate, and is it applied to BioTest's corpus too? If the validity gate filters baseline corpora more stringently than BioTest's, that is an unfair-recipe artefact.
- **Vargha–Delaney effect-size labels missing:** the paper reports $\hat{A}_{12}$ values but does not cite the small/medium/large convention ($0.56/0.64/0.71$), so a reader cannot translate $\hat{A}_{12}{=}0.25$ into a magnitude judgment. Vargha & Delaney (2000) is missing from the bibliography.
- **PIT's "reachable" denominator** is conflated with engine semantics. PIT defines reachable mutants as those that the test suite executes; mutmut's "reachable" definition is materially different. The paper's `reachable = killed + survived + timed-out` formula is PIT-style and is *not* universally how the four engines define adequacy.
- **Conclusion overpromises future work**: claims a "per-SUT coverage-feedback extension … would close most of the biopython gap … on the theory of Padhye et al. 2019." Padhye's Zest does not provide that prediction; the conclusion presents speculation as theoretical entailment.
- **Anonymous identifier slip:** `documents/Flow.md` and the artefact's `compares/results/bug_bench/PHASE4_FINAL_REPORT.md` contain absolute Windows paths under `C:\Users\miaot\...`. If the artefact is shared at submission, this de-anonymises.
- **English/typography:** abstract line 31 uses a non-breaking space inside math mode that may not render correctly across IEEEtran's `cite` package; the small-caps macro `\biotest{}` is inconsistent with the bibliography's plain-text "BioTest" in `note` fields.
- **Background section is encyclopaedic.** §II.A reproduces what every reader already knows about VCF and SAM. A two-page background in a 10-page conference paper is presentation slack.

## 5. Specific questions the authors must answer in the rebuttal

1. *Real-bug benchmark.* The artefact contains `compares/results/bug_bench/PHASE4_FINAL_REPORT.md` reporting BioTest finds 13/32 manifest bugs vs. Atheris/Jazzer/libFuzzer/cargo-fuzz. Why is this benchmark excluded from the paper, and would the authors report the full table in the rebuttal? Without it, the construct-validity gap (M1) is not closeable.
2. *Kill-aware corpus minimisation.* `corpus_minimize.py::minimize_by_kills` uses mutation-kill information to greedy-select the corpus that is then graded by the same mutation engine. Why is this not circular? If the response is "Vikram et al. ISSTA'23 used the same idea," that paper used kill-feedback as a *fuzzer guidance* signal, not as an *evaluation* signal. The paper must either disable `minimize_by_kills` or quantify its contribution to the headline numbers.
3. *Identical kill semantics vs. oracle-strength asymmetry.* If the kill predicate (per the fairness recipe) is computed by the mutation engine on every tool's corpus uniformly, how does BioTest's "stronger oracle" produce the seqan3 +6.87pp lead? The lead must live in the corpus or in the kill-predicate itself; specify which.
4. *MR cardinality and contribution.* How many distinct MRs are active in the headline runs per format? How many were authored by humans vs. mined by the LLM? What is the kill rate when only the LLM-mined MRs are active? Without these numbers the LLM-as-MR-mining claim is not measurable.
5. *Thirteen-rank ablation.* Report mutation score on each of the six cells using only Ranks 1–7 (metamorphic), then 1–11 (metamorphic + structured-non-metamorphic), then 1–13 (full). The paper currently reports only the full configuration. The single-cell biopython ablation is not enough.
6. *Iteration-count parity.* Re-run seqan3/SAM and biopython/SAM under iteration-count parity (identical number of corpus inputs per cell) instead of wall-clock parity. If the seqan3 lead and the biopython deficit both shrink, the budget recipe is the problem; if they hold, the recipe is fair on those cells.

## 6. Verdict + score breakdown

- **Novelty: 2/5.** The combination of LLM-mined MRs + 13-rank stack + multi-runner consensus is a packaging contribution. None of the components is individually novel; together they are a competent integration, not a new technique.
- **Technical rigor: 1/5.** $n{=}4$ with $\sigma{=}0.00$ on multiple cells, kill-aware cmin not disclosed in the paper, kill semantics not reconciled with the seqan3 oracle-strength claim, Pure-Random floor that calibrates parser strictness rather than recipe quality.
- **Empirical strength: 2/5.** Six cells, two text formats, real-bug benchmark withheld. The mutation-adequacy table is competently produced but answers the wrong question for a top-tier audience.
- **Reproducibility: 2/5.** Single Docker image is claimed but no public registry URL; artefact contains absolute paths under an identifying user directory; corpus-minimisation strategy is undocumented in the paper.
- **Presentation: 3/5.** TikZ pipeline figure is the right idea, executed with too many moving parts. Threats section is honest about most issues, which paradoxically makes it easier for the reviewer to articulate the rejection.
